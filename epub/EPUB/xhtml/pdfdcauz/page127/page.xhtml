ZERO CONDITIONAL

It is used to talk about things that never change.

If you eat junk food, you get fat  
If + simple present...simple present

When you touch fire, you get burned.

FIRST CONDITIONAL

It is used to refer to something that is possible to happen in the  
present 
or  
future 
.

If Sam gets the job, he will move to Australia

If + Present tense  
Future “will”

SUFFIXES FOR NOUNS AND VERBS

A suffix is a letter or group of letters added at the end of a word which makes a new word.

NOUN (THING) ENDINGS: 
-ism, -ment, -nce, -ness, -ion, -ty, -age, -ship

NOUN (PERSON) ENDINGS: 
-er, -or, -ist, -cian

VERB ENDINGS:  
-en, -ate, -ize, -ify

Unit 6

PRESENT PERFECT SIMPLE

Have (in the simple present) + Verb (in the past participle form)

Affirmative Negative  
Interrogative

I have worked.  
I have not worked.  
Have you worked?

Contractions

I have worked = I've worked

He has worked = He's worked  
I have not worked = I haven't worked

He has not worked = He hasn’t worked

Already, Since, For, Yet 
are often used with the present perfect.

Have you written the report yet?

No, I haven't written the report yet.

Yes, I have already written the report.

I have known her for a long time.

I have known her since 2007.

PRESENT PERFECT VS. SIMPLE PAST

You must always use the Present perfect when the time of an action is not important or not specified.

You must always use the Simple past when details about the time or place that an action occurred

are given or requested.

Present perfect  
Simple past

I have lived in Lyon.  
I lived in Lyon in 1989.

They have eaten Thai food.  
They ate Thai food last night.

Have you seen 'Othello'?  
Where did you see 'Othello'?

We have been to Ireland.  
When did you go to Ireland?
